# Pre-Mortem — Reference

Inline reference for the `situation-analysis` skill. Not a standalone skill.

## Core concept

Gary Klein's pre-mortem: imagine the initiative has already failed. Work backward from the failure to identify what went wrong. This is more effective than standard risk assessment because it gives people permission to voice concerns they'd normally suppress ("I don't want to be the one who kills the project's momentum").

## The procedure

1. **Set the scene.** "It's [future date — end of the initiative timeline]. The initiative has failed. Not a partial success, not a 'we learned a lot' — it failed. The goal was not met."

2. **Generate failure modes.** Ask: "What went wrong? What caused it to fail?" Capture every scenario the user names. Push for at least 5. The goal is breadth, not depth — get all the failure modes on the table before evaluating any of them.

3. **Categorize.** Group failure modes into clusters:
   - **Execution failures** — the team didn't do the work (capacity, skill, attention)
   - **Assumption failures** — a core assumption proved false (market, customer, technical)
   - **External shocks** — something outside the team's control changed (competitor, regulation, macro)
   - **Design failures** — the plan itself was flawed (wrong target, wrong lever, wrong sequencing)

4. **Rank by likelihood × impact.** For each failure mode: how likely is it (1–5), and how severe if it happens (1–5)? Multiply for a rough priority score. Focus mitigation on the top 3–5.

5. **Design mitigations.** For each high-priority failure mode: what specific action reduces the likelihood or limits the damage? Mitigations must be concrete and assignable — "be careful" is not a mitigation; "add a weekly pipeline review checkpoint with Claire" is.

## Quality rules

- **Failure modes must be specific.** "Execution risk" is not a failure mode. "Seth's strategists are already at capacity and can't take on expansion conversations without dropping existing client work" is.
- **Don't comfort the user.** The pre-mortem is supposed to be uncomfortable. If the user only generates optimistic failure modes ("we grew too fast!"), push: "What's the scenario where you don't reach the target at all?"
- **Include the uncomfortable ones.** The most valuable failure modes are the ones people don't want to say out loud — key-person risk, founder disagreements, customer concentration, market-not-as-big-as-we-think.
- **Mitigations must be pre-commitments, not reactions.** "If X happens, we'll figure it out" is not a mitigation. "Before we launch, we'll [specific action] to reduce the chance of X" is.

## Presentation format

Present as: (1) the failure scenario framing, (2) full list of failure modes grouped by category, (3) top 5 ranked by likelihood × impact as a table, (4) mitigations for each with owner and timing.
