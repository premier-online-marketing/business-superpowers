# Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) — Reference

Inline reference for the `situation-analysis` skill. Not a standalone skill.

## Core concept

Customers don't buy products or services — they "hire" them to make progress on a specific job in their life or work. Understanding the job reveals why customers switch, what they value, and where competitive threats actually come from.

## The job statement format

A job statement follows this structure:

> **When** [situation/trigger], **I want to** [motivation/action], **so I can** [desired outcome].

Examples for a digital marketing agency:

> **When** I take over marketing for a new property, **I want to** get occupancy leads flowing quickly, **so I can** show my regional director measurable results within 90 days.

> **When** my portfolio grows beyond what my team can manage in-house, **I want to** hand off SEO/PPC to a specialist vendor, **so I can** focus my team on strategy and resident experience.

## Quality rules

- **The job is stable.** "Get occupancy leads" is a job that existed before digital marketing and will exist after it. Don't confuse the job with the current solution.
- **The job belongs to the customer, not the company.** Write from the customer's perspective. "Grow our agency" is the company's goal, not the customer's job.
- **Situation matters as much as motivation.** The "when" clause is what differentiates jobs — the same person has different jobs in different situations.
- **Outcomes must be measurable.** "So I can feel confident" is too vague. "So I can show measurable results within 90 days" is specific.
- **Include functional, emotional, and social dimensions:**
  - Functional: "get leads flowing"
  - Emotional: "reduce anxiety about portfolio performance"
  - Social: "look competent to my regional director"

## Interview-mode questions

When the skill is being used to discover jobs (not just frame known ones):

1. "When did you first start looking for a solution like this? What was happening?"
2. "What were you using before? What was frustrating about it?"
3. "What made you finally switch / hire / buy? What was the trigger?"
4. "What would you miss most if this went away tomorrow?"
5. "Who else did you consider? Why did you choose this over them?"

## Presentation format

Present as: (1) the primary job statement, (2) 2–3 secondary jobs that coexist, (3) the competitive landscape reframed through jobs (who else is "hired" for the same job), (4) implications for the company's positioning and messaging.
